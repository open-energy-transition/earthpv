Why nothing measured crosses 180° — and the mirrored half

Sentinel-2 crosses this latitude at a fixed ~10:30 local time, so across two full years of imagery the sun's azimuth at the moment of every overpass never once swings west of due south — a panel facing southwest, west, or north cannot glint into this sensor no matter how long you wait. That's a sensor limit, not a property of Pakistani rooftops, so the dashed outline points beyond 180° are this same measured sample mirrored across the south axis: assuming installers have no inherent reason to favour installations offset east of south over the equivalent offset west of south (both are driven by roof/land constraints, not solar geometry), the true population should be roughly symmetric. These points are illustrative, not measured — no installation was actually detected there, and mirroring only recovers the assumed shape, not the identity or exact count of any real west-facing installation. The residual shaded wedge (now only ~137° instead of 180°) is what stays unreachable even granting the assumption.

‡ Checked directly: of 5,730 real Lahore building footprints, 31.2% have neither possible panel-facing direction (perpendicular to the roof's long axis, since a row of panels faces away from its own mounting edge, not along it) inside the achievable window — nearly a third of rooftops could never validate under this method, independent of data volume.

† A single spike can't be checked for self-consistency — 17.6% of installations glint once (or glint on dates that disagree on a panel geometry) and so are correctly detected as "PV is probably here" but carry no orientation information. Don't mistake this project's own reported "51% detected" figures for a pose sample size — detection and pose-fitting use different, non-interchangeable criteria.
